What I do

Three services. One specialty.

Every engagement starts with the same question: where is this restaurant actually bleeding, and what do we stop first?

01

Concept Development

Napkin sketch → opening night
▾

From napkin sketch to opening night. I build concepts that hold together — brand, menu, space, and economics as a single, coherent thing. The goal is to open the doors on a restaurant that makes money on day one, not to chase an aesthetic that collapses under its own cost structure.

  • Concept positioning & brand architecture
  • Menu engineering & pricing strategy
  • Space & flow consulting
  • Vendor sourcing & opening playbook
  • Pre-opening P&L modeling
02

Operations Turnaround

Open & bleeding → Find the cut
▾

When a restaurant is open but hemorrhaging, I come in. Labor, food cost, prime cost, systems, management — we fix what's actually broken, not what's loudest. Most operators know something is wrong; fewer know which number is the real problem. We find it, fix it, and build the systems so it doesn't come back.

  • Prime cost audit & remediation
  • Labor scheduling & productivity
  • Food cost & waste reduction
  • SOPs, line structure, & KDS setup
  • Management coaching & accountability systems
03

Investor Readiness

Deck → Second meeting → Signed
▾

If you're raising, your deck better hold up in the second meeting. I pressure-test the pro forma, sharpen the story, and prepare you for the questions you don't want to answer. Investors hear the pitch. The smart ones ask about the margin at unit three. We get you ready for them.

  • Pro forma & unit economics modeling
  • Pitch deck review & rebuild
  • Investor Q&A prep
  • Multi-unit scaling plans
  • Data rooms & due-diligence packages
How I work

Direct, focused, and grounded in the numbers.

If you want a consultant who tells you what you want to hear, I'm the wrong call. Here's what you get instead.

01

Restaurants are specific. Specialists win.

Focus is the edge

I don't advise hotels, bars, ghost kitchens, or catering operations. Restaurants are their own business — different economics, different labor structure, different guest psychology. Operators who hire generalists get generalist advice. You deserve the operator in the room who's already lived the problem.

02

The numbers don't lie.

Every assessment starts with your P&L

Every assessment starts and ends with the financials. Prime cost, labor, food, rent, occupancy — we look at what's real before we talk about what's possible. Feelings are useful for the dining room, not for the fix.

03

Honest assessments, even when they hurt.

Soft feedback is worse than none

If your concept isn't viable, I'll tell you. If your pitch deck is oversold, I'll tell you. If you're one year from closure without serious changes, I'll tell you that too. You aren't paying me to agree with you — you're paying me to save you from the version of this that ends badly.

04

Implementation, not just recommendations.

Strategy in a PDF is theater

Every engagement includes an execution plan and accountability checkpoints. We don't wrap up the work when the report is written — we wrap up when the systems are running, the operators know how to use them, and the numbers are starting to move.

The Operator

30 years on the line.

I'm Justise — the founder and the only consultant. I've spent three decades in restaurants: line cook to chef to GM to operator. I've opened concepts, rebuilt broken ones, and sat on the operator side of investor calls that went well and ones that didn't.

Back of House Strategies is a deliberately narrow practice. Restaurants are their own animal. The economics are brutal, the margins are thin, and the operators who win are the ones who actually understand what's happening in the kitchen at 7:47 on a Saturday. Generalist consultants don't. I do.

I take a small number of engagements a year so the ones I do take get the work they deserve.
